# Professional basis and scope

These sources define the safeguards used by the skill. They are not sources of copied code.

- Nature Portfolio image-integrity policy requires minimal processing, whole-image/equal-control adjustments, disclosure of pseudocoloring and nonlinear adjustments, retention of unprocessed files, and prohibition of cloning/healing or biased local emphasis: https://www.nature.com/nature/editorial-policies/image-integrity
- Nature's figure guide requires RGB image delivery, adequate native resolution, editable text, and scale bars/text on separate editable layers rather than flattened into the image: https://research-figure-guide.nature.com/figures/preparing-figures-our-specifications/
- The community-developed Nature Methods checklists cover light-microscopy image formatting/annotation, color selection, data availability, and reporting of image-analysis workflows: https://doi.org/10.1038/s41592-023-01987-9
- Rossner and Yamada describe why digital manipulation, selective enhancement, and misleading juxtaposition compromise scientific images: https://doi.org/10.1083/jcb.200406019
- The Open Microscopy Environment provides the open data model and OME-TIFF/OME-XML standards used to preserve microscopy pixels and acquisition metadata such as physical pixel size: https://www.openmicroscopy.org/docs/
- The MDAR framework links materials, experimental design, analysis, and transparent reporting across life-science publishers: https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2103238118

## Deliberate boundary

This skill prepares display derivatives and publication panels. It does not segment objects, quantify intensities, remove background, deconvolve, stitch, register, denoise, sharpen, or infer calibration. Those operations require modality-specific methods, validation, and a separate quantitative workflow. Proprietary microscopy files should first be read with metadata-aware software such as Bio-Formats; a screenshot is not an authoritative calibrated source.
